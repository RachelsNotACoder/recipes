Hoisin-Glazed Vietnamese Beef Meatballs

Prep / Total 20m / 45m
Tags
Sheetpan Asian Beef
Servings
6

Food-processor beef meatballs, baked hands-off and tossed in a fish sauce-hoisin glaze, served alongside quick-pickled carrots and cucumber.

Ingredients

Preparation

  1. 01

    Combine carrots and cucumbers with the vinegar and sugar to marinate. You can do this in advance, or you can just throw everything together before you start work on the meatballs. Either way works.

  2. 02

    Preheat the oven to 425 degrees and line two rimmed sheet pans with foil for easy cleanup.

  3. 03

    Combine all the ingredients for the meatballs in your food processor. Pulse until everything is broken down and blended. You may have to scrape the sides down a couple of times in the early going. Sometimes it can be helpful to just put in the vegetable parts first and get them down to a paste or pesto, then add the meat, but it works if you just throw everything in, too. So easy!

  4. 04

    Divide meat mixture into 24 portions and shape into meatballs. (I like to keep a bowl of cool water handy and dip my hands in frequently to help keep the meat mixture from sticking too much to my hands.) Place the meatballs on the sheet pan in a single layer.

  5. 05

    Place the sheet pan in the oven and roast for 15-20 minutes, until they have set up.

  6. 06

    Combine the ingredients for the sauce in a small to medium saucepan and set on medium low heat. You may want to add a tablespoon or two of water if you will have it on the stove a long time, but in any case it won't be a lot of sauce.

  7. 07

    When you remove the meatballs from the oven, plop them in the warm sauce and toss to coat them with it thoroughly.

  8. 08

    Serve with the marinated vegetables alone, with lettuce wraps, or with rice noodles.

Tips and Notes

  • This recipe is adapted from The Pickled Plum. When I first made it, I was a little skeptical, since it breaks all the meatball rules: no bread binder, no egg, go ahead and overwork the meat mixture, etc. I was sure they'd turn out tough. Imagine my surprise when they didn't! These turned out just the right texture for me.
  • Personally, I am not a fan of frying meatballs: it seems labor intensive and messy compared to throwing them on a sheet pan and leaving them alone for a bit. But if you are a fan of fried meatballs, of course you can do that here. The original recipe on The Pickled Plum does indeed call for frying.
  • Usually for a meatball, burger, or meatloaf, you would want to use a higher fat ground beef, say 80/20. (I often grind my own from a cheap chuck roast). When I made this, the ground beef I had on hand and use was very lean: about 93/7. This was another reason I was expecting tough meatballs, and was surprised they didn't turn out that way! But in retrospect, I think it was because these are baked: higher fat meatballs, when baked, release a lot of grease onto the tray, and that actually can cause them to end up cooking faster, almost "frying" in the released fat.
  • Fair warning: this is one of those foods that smells great while you're cooking it, tastes great when you're eating it, but leftovers will stink your refrigerator up. If you don't have immediate plans for your leftovers, it might be worthwhile to freeze them instead to minimize that. Or you know, just eat them all up right away!
