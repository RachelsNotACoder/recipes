Chicken Paprikash and Hungarian Baked Cauliflower

Prep / Total 15m / 45m
Tags
Crockpot E Europe Chicken
Servings
6

Classic chicken paprikash braised in a paprika-soy pan sauce, paired with a vege-forward Hungarian side of roasted cauliflower baked under a buttered panko-yogurt crust.

Ingredients

Preparation

  1. 01

    Preheat oven to 400’ degrees. Prepare a large baking dish by spraying with a light coat of oil, then put the cauliflower pieces in it. Bake, uncovered, for 30 minutes.

  2. 02

    Combine the panko crumbs and butter and set aside for later.

  3. 03

    Heat a large lidded skillet on medium high heat. Add the one tablespoon of the oil and chicken pieces and brown on all sides, about 5 minutes. Remove chicken to a separate plate.

  4. 04

    Reduce the heat to medium and add the rest of the oil with the onions and peppers. Cook, stirring frequently to scrape up brown bits from the bottom of the pan, about 5 minutes.

  5. 05

    Add the paprika and garlic and cook, stirring constantly, until the aromas bloom, about 1 minute. Stir in the flour first, then pour in the chicken broth with the soy sauce. Stir to combine, then reduce the heat to medium low and nestle the chicken back into the pan with the vegetables and sauce. Cook on medium low until the liquid has reduced and the sauce has thickened, about 15 minutes.

  6. 06

    While the chicken is on its final braising step above, remove the cauliflower from the oven and toss it with the yogurt (or the cultured non-dairy milk). Return to the baking dish and sprinkle with the buttered panko crumbs plus some extra paprika to garnish. Place under the broiler for 5 minutes to turn the crumbs a golden brown and serve with the chicken paprikash.

Tips and Notes

  • Okay, so chicken paprikash is usually served with egg noodles (or occasionally, potatoes), and you might totally do that. However, I wanted to give you a more vege-tastic option, so I looked around for Hungarian veggie dishes that would go well with it. This cauliflower does the trick, and it's quite tasty. Many recipes would call for steaming the cauliflower, but I just love it roasted, always. More traditionally it would be coated in sour cream, but yogurt always makes a good substitute for that and is something many people just keep on hand nowadays. Plus it's usually higher in protein (though I can't speak for the non dairy alternatives).
  • “Soy sauce in a Hungarian dish? Really?” you ask. Yes! All you really need is an umami bomb to make the sauce pop (it won't taste like soy sauce, I promise), and in the modern American kitchen, soy sauce is the most commonly found one. Plus it keeps well, etc. If the idea really bothers you, you can substitute a couple of tablespoons of anchovy paste or just a few mashed up anchovies straight from the can -- same difference, really.
  • I know I often call for smoked paprika, but this is a case where regular paprika (sometimes called “sweet” paprika) is what you really want. Smoked paprika is delicious, but tends to go better with Mediterranean dishes, and sweet paprika is more authentic here.
