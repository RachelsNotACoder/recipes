West African Peanut and Chicken Crockpot Curry

Prep / Total 15m / 6h 15m
Tags
Crockpot African Chicken
Servings
6

A slow cooker take on West African groundnut stew — chicken and sweet potatoes simmer in a curried tomato broth, finished with peanut butter and chard.

Ingredients

Preparation

  1. 01

    Layer the crockpot with onions at the bottom, chicken over it, sweet potatoes on top. Pour the curry powder or spice mix on top with the ginger, garlic, and jalapeno.

  2. 02

    Mix the tomato paste with some of the stock (just to break it down) and add it with the rest of the stock to the crockpot. Set the crockpot to low and cook for about 5 hours.

  3. 03

    After 5 hours, stir in the peanut butter, then top with the chard. The chard will not be submerged in the stew at this point, and depending on the size of your crockpot you may have to press it down a bit to make sure it's all in the crock. The lid should hold it down. Recover and continue cooking on low for another 1 hour.

  4. 04

    After an hour the chard should be cooked down so you can stir it into the stew. Serve with chopped cilantro and peanuts.

Tips and Notes

  • This curry is a slow cooker variation on West African Groundnut Stew. There are many recipes for this popular stew on the internet, but if you want to try a non-crockpot version that is ready quicker (it's much more labor intensive, though), a similar one is very similar and takes about an hour or so.
  • With the exception of garam masala, I don't tend to keep pre-blended spice mixes on hand. I find that I can use them in such a limited number of recipes that they don't turnover on my shelf before they expire. And when I encounter a recipe calling for “curry powder” I'll often get all anxious because I think that can mean some pretty widely different things. So I generally prefer to make my own blends, and in this case I've provided a substitution for “curry powder.” You can probably use more turmeric than I did -- I don't love cleaning turmeric stains out of things in my kitchen (and I've ended up having to dedicate a few specific utensils to curries so I don't permanently stain all my storage containers, wooden spoons, spatulas, etc.) so I'll often cut back on it.
  • I'm a big fan of chard, partly because after you stem it, you can treat the stems like a whole other vegetable to serve with another dish (I like them roasted with olive oil and cheese -- but then, what don't I like that way?) but kale or collard greens make great substitutions here if you prefer. I'd stay away from more tender greens like spinach though, as they'll just break down to mush.
  • Garlic and ginger root -- as many of you know I use this combination often enough that I usually just have a pre-made 50-50 blended paste of it in cubes in my freezer. 1 ½ tbsp of ginger-garlic paste should about do the trick here.
